\begin{align*} \sup \left\{ \| M x \|_{Y} \middle| \| x \|_{X} < 1 \right\} &\leq \sup \left\{ \frac{1}{\| x \|_{X}} \| M x \|_{Y} \middle| \| x \|_{X} < 1 \right\} \\ &= \sup \left\{ \| M \frac{x}{\| x \|_{X}} \|_{Y} \middle| \| x \|_{X} < 1 \right\} \\ &= \sup \left\{ \| M u \|_{Y} \middle| \| u \|_{X} = 1 \right\} \\ &\leq \sup \left\{ \| M u \|_{Y} \middle| \| u \|_{X} \leq 1 \right\} \\ &\leq \sup \left\{ \frac{\| M u \|_{Y}}{\| u\|_{X}} \middle| \| u \|_{X} \leq 1 \right\} \\ &\leq \sup \left\{ \frac{\| M u \|_{Y}}{\| u\|_{X}} \middle| \| u \|_{X} \neq 0 \right\} \end{align*}

Because the supremum is a limit point, there is a sequence,

\[ \frac{\| M u_{n} \|_{Y}}{\| u_{n} \|_{X}} \to \sup_{\| u \|_{X}} \frac{\| M u \|_{Y}}{\| u \|_{X}} \]

Consider the sequence, \( y_{n} = \frac{n}{n+1} \frac{u_{n}}{\| u_{n}\|_{X}} \)

Then,

\begin{align*} \lim_{n \to \infty} \| M y_{n} \|_{Y} &= \lim_{n \to \infty} \left\| M \left(\frac{n}{n+1} \frac{u_{n}}{\| u_{n}\|_{X}} \right) \right\|_{Y} \\ &= \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{n}{n+1} \frac{\left\| M u_{n} \right\|_{Y}}{\| u_{n} \|_{X}} \\ &= \sup_{\| u \|_{X}} \frac{\| M u \|_{Y}}{\| u \|_{X}} \end{align*}

By construction \( \| y_{n} \| < 1 \), yet attain the same supremum. Hence,

\[ \sup \left\{ \| M x \|_{Y} \middle| \| x \|_{X} < 1 \right\} \geq \sup \left\{ \frac{\| M u \|_{Y}}{\| u\|_{X}} \middle| \| u \|_{X} \neq 0 \right\} \]

Allowing us to deduce the equivalence of the first four definitions of the operator norm.

We may prove that the last two definitions are equivalent.

\begin{align*} M(B_{X}(0,1)) \subset \overline{B_{Y}}(0,r) &\iff \forall \| x \| < 1 \colon \| Mx \| \leq r &\quad &\text{Element wise Definition} \\ &\implies \sup_{\| x\| < 1} \| Mx \| \leq r &\quad &\text{Take Supremum} \\ &\implies \sup_{\| x\| < 1} \| Mx \| \leq \inf_{r \colon M(B_{X}(0,1)) \subset \overline{B_{Y}}(0,r)} r &\quad &\text{Take Infimum} \\ &\iff \sup_{\| x\| \leq 1} \| M x \| \leq \inf_{r \colon M(B_{X}(0,1)) \subset \overline{B_{Y}}(0,r)} r &\quad &\text{Equivalance of Operator Norm} \end{align*}

We will prove equality by supposing for the sake of contradiction, that the inequality is strict.

\[ \sup_{\| x\| \leq 1} \| Mx \| < \inf_{r \colon M(B_{X}(0,1)) \subset \overline{B_{Y}}(0,r)} r = r_{m} \]

This says that for all unit \( x \), there is some \( \varepsilon > 0 \) such that,

\[ \| M x \| + \varepsilon < r_{m} \implies \| M x \| < r_{m} - \varepsilon \]

But this means there is an \( x \in B_{X}(0,1) \) that gets mapped to a ball of smaller radius \( r_{m} - \varepsilon < r_{m} \) which contradicts minimality of \( r_{m} \). To see why the infimum is a minimum, we may show that the set which defines the infimum is a closed set. We show this by proving its complement is open:

\begin{align*} \left\{ r \in \mathbb{R} \colon M(B_{X}(0,1)) \subset \overline{B_{Y}}(0,r) \right\}^{c} &= \left\{ r \in \mathbb{R} \colon \forall \| x \| < 1 \colon \| Mx \| \leq r \right\}^{c} \\ &= \left\{ r \in \mathbb{R} \colon \exists \| x \| < 1 \colon \| Mx \| > r \right\} \end{align*}

By continuity of \( M \) we see that this set is open (if a continuous function is strictly larger than some value then there is a neighborhood in which it is larger than that value).

Thus,

\[ \inf_{r \colon M(B_{X}(0,1)) \subset \overline{B_{Y}}(0,r)} r = \min_{r \colon M(B_{X}(0,1)) \subset \overline{B_{Y}}(0,r)} r \]

Suppose \( \phi \neq 0 \) is a continuous linear functional on \( L_{p}([0,1]) \) for \( 0 < p < 1 \).

Because \( \phi \neq 0 \) it has nonzero operator norm. Hence, there is some \( f \) with \( \| f \|_{p} = 1 \) such that \( 0 < \phi f =: \alpha \).

Because \( \| f \|_{p} = 1 \) we have

\[ \left(\int_{0}^{1} |f|^{p} dx\right)^{1/p} = 1 \iff \int_{0}^{1} |f|^{p} dx = 1 \]

We know that

\[ g(x) = \int_{0}^{x} |f|^{p} dx \]

is a continuous function with \( g(0) = 0 \) and \( g(1) = 1 \). By IVT we see that there is some \( s \) such that \( g(s) = \int_{0}^{s} |f|^{p} dx = 1/2 \). By additivity of domain of integration bounds we see that

\[ \int_{s}^{1} |f|^{p} dx = 1/2 \]

We will repeat this procedure of splitting the interval into halves. At the \( i \)-th step, we have \( 2^{i} \) many intervals, \( [s_{i,j}, s_{i,j+1}] \) such that \( f \), has norm \( \int_{s_{i,j}}^{s_{i,j+1}} |f|^{p} dx = \frac{1}{2^{i}} \) on that interval.

Let

\[ f_{i,j}(x) = \begin{cases} f(x) & x \in [s_{i,j},s_{i,j + 1}] \\ 0 & \text{else} \end{cases} \]

Then we may represent \( f \) as a sum \( f = \sum_{j = 1}^{2^{i}} f_{i,j} \)

\[ \alpha = \phi f = \phi \sum f_{j} = \sum \phi f_{j} \]

There are \( 2^{j} \) terms in this sum adding to \( \alpha \). Hence there is some element \( f_{i,j} \) such that \( \phi f_{i,j} > 2^{-i} \alpha \).

Hence,

\[ \phi f_{i,j} > 2^{-i} \alpha > 2^{-i / p} \alpha = \| f_{i,j} \|_{p} \alpha \]

Which shows that the operator is not continuous.
