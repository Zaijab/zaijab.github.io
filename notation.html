2. Limits in Analysis

A sequence is a function \( a \colon \mathbb{N} \to \mathbb{R} \) denoted \( a_n \).

A sequence has a limit when

\[ \exists a \in \mathbb{R} \forall \varepsilon > 0 \exists N \in \mathbb{N} \forall n \geq N . | a_n - a | < \varepsilon \]

We may denote this succinctly as,

\[ a_n \to a \]

Building from above, if we do write \( b_n \to b \), we will assume \( b_n \) is a sequence in some space obvious from context, and \( b \) is the limit of the sequence.

3. Set Builder Notation

Given a predicate \( P \colon U \to 1 \) we may consider the collection of all objects satisfying the predicate, denoted by:

\[ \{ x \mid P(x) \} \]

As stated, this collection need not be a set. In most circumstances, we will use the axiom schema of comprehension which states that the following is a set given a set \( X \)

\[ \{ x \in X \mid P(x) \} \]

When obvious from context we will omit the superset \( X \).

6. Non Formal Sentences

The writing that takes place in mathematics is

Mathematical proofs are designed to be written in a way that is so precise that a computer may verify the proof. We humans are not computers, and perhaps it is unhelpful from a pedagogical point of view to humans as such. Here is a sentence that would not fly in modern writing:

\( f \colon s(f) \to t(f) \)

The reason for this definition not making sense is because we are defining \( f \) in terms of its own source and target. That is, the definition is self referential without any base case. The "true" way to write out such an object is,

\[ f \colon X \to Y \]

where \( X = s(f) \) and \( Y \supseteq t(f) \).

9. Maps

We take all maps to have a specified domain and range, that is, the following expression makes sense:

\[ f \colon \dom(f) \to \ran(f) \]

Strictly speaking, from the definitions of functions we do not have the above setup a priori. However, we may take the domain and range of an arbitrary function \( g \) and redefine \( f \) to be the correct domain and range from the arbitrary function.

10. Style Guide

I really enjoy the intuitive feel for why a subject ought to be true. Reading and doing math is a portal to understanding the universe at a more fundamental level. Part of Math's power is the ability to rigorously check every statement for correctness. This is also part of the difficulty in teaching math. People, especially authors and Math StackExchange users, feel the need for every single statement ever made should be a correct one. I find this notion against the role of a mathematician and is rarely done in full technicality. How many books outline the specific inaccessible cardinal the author works over to make all of the statements true? True rigor is hard to come by. The Principia Mathematica attempts to write in a style which does include all of the rigor however it is far too terse and unproductive to read or write books like those. There are still a number of famous theorems not yet formalized in theorem provers like the Prime Number Theorem and the Classification of Finite Simple Groups.

Pure mathematicians dislike the treatment of derivatives as fractions. But the notation is very suggestive and creates many mnemonics for rules pertaining to calculations. (See separation of variables, total derivatives, parameterizations, inverse function theorem). Of course there is a balance that needs to be attained. In my writing, I will keep a lot of the intuition and build up the ideas for the definitions, theorems, and examples. Each line will be justified in two ways, intuitive and rigorously. Intuitive and short arguments will be kept in the main text. Whereas the rigorous (perhaps unhelpful) proofs will be put in the summary section along with the rigorous definitions.

To this end, there will be two sections outlining the material. 1. Prose, in which I detail how the material fits into a larger narrative and its current applications. 2. Summary, the end of the section will have a summary of part 1. This section, when viewed as an Org mode file, will have each definition and theorem marked as a Flashcard and you may then review using an SRS system within Emacs.
