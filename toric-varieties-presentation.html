1.1. Geometry with Perspective

Slogan: Projective Geometry is geometry with perspective, the good and the bad.

1.1.1. The Good

Have you ever seen monkeys reacting to magic? Or how will we react to this topological puzzle.

What is the one thing we all do when we are trying to get a handle on a confusing looking object? We move it around and look at it from all sorts of different angles. Hopefully one of the angles or the movement itself will let us understand how the object truly sits in space.

1.1.2. The Bad

Suppose we were watching the night sky and we were looking at the moon. One may ask, how big is the moon? My answer? Very small, I can cover it just using the pencil eraser hence it's smaller than the eraser… Right? Of course not.

Perspective makes it impossible to gauge size correctly.

1.1.4. The Cross Ratio

One thing that is always preserved under a change of perspective is known as the cross ratio.

Given 4 points \( A,B,C,D \) we can compute the cross ratio as \( \frac{AC \cdot BD}{BC \cdot AD} \)

Given an origin (center of perspectivity) \( O \) when lines passing through \( O \) and \( A,B,C,D \) then hitting another line \( \ell \) gives \( A',B',C',D' \) where \( (A,B,C,D) = (A',B',C',D') \) (they have the same cross ratio).

This is important in constructing a grid.

If we start with 3 evenly spaced points on a line the 4th point which makes the cross ratio minus one is the point at infinity. We can then construct a grid by taking two points on the horrizon and making a grid using the intersections from one point at infinity and a point on the original line and another point at infinity. We can then compute a lot of ideas related to the horrizon using the cross ratio.

1.1.9. Calculations

We have said that the parabola and hyperbola intersects the line at infinity. Where does it intersect? How can we calculate it? Also, does the hyperbola extend beyond infinity?

We use something called homogeneous coordinates.

First, let us understand a geometrically isomorphic construction to our 'perspective' plane.

First let's embed our line into the plane. Note: every point on the line can be paired with a line through the origin. So there is a correspondence between the range of points on the line and the pencil of lines through the origin. Except for one line, the line y=0 is not reached in this way. This will correspond to the point at infinity. This is justified by taking the limits of lines approaching infinity.

The idea is then to make our coordinate system for the projective space be homogeneous coordinates. That is, \( [x \colon y] = [kx \colon ky] \) as the point is a stand in for the line passing through the point. And scaling a point by any nonzero multiple yields the same line.

We can make a similar construction for the plane First take the lines from the origin going through the plane \( z = 1 \). The lines hit every point on the plane but horrizontal lines on \( z = 0 \) are not hit. By taking a planar intersection this reduces down to a 1 dimensional image of what we've already seen before. So we can consider the point at infinity in the direction of the line \( [x \colon y \colon 0] \). Connecting all points at infinity together yields the line at infinity.

1.1.10. Homogenization

To calculate where the parabola intersects infinity describe the equation

\( y = x^2 \) then homogenize it, (this is the embedding into the higher dimensional space)

\( yz = x^2 \) we multiply by \( z \) until all the degrees are the same. This is to make the equation well behaved using pencils.

To see where it intersects the line at infinity, set \( z=0 \) giving \( 0 = x^2 \) so \( x=0 \) and thus \( y \) is free. So we get the homogeneous coordinate \( [0 \colon y \colon 0] = [0 \colon 1 \colon 0] \). So the parabola intersects infinity at the infinity in the direction of the y-axis.

With the hyperbola \( y^2 = 6x^2 - 5yz \) we get homogeneous coordinate solutions \( [-1 \colon \sqrt{6} \colon 0], [1 \colon \sqrt{6} \colon 0] \)

Using homogeneous coordinates we now know what happens after infinity. The line connecting infinity wraps around to the negative numbers. So the ellipse wrapped around infinity to the other side giving us two parts to the hyperbola.

Again, the hyperbola is just an ellipse with perspective. It looks discontinuous because the ellipse actually wraps around infinity and reaches back into the normal plane. That's why we have two branches to the hyperbola but one shape.

Another calculation,

where does \( x^2 + y^2 = 1 \) intersect infinity?

It might appear that it won't but just redo the process.

Homogenize: \( x^2 + y^2 = z^2 \) Solve: \( x^2 + y^2 = 0 \implies [x \colon ix \colon 0], [x \colon -ix \colon 0] \)

1.1.11. Bezout's Theorem

If two algebraic curves of degrees \( m \) and \( n \) have no common components. Then it has exactly \( mn \) points of intersection if we use complex numbers, multiplicities, and points at infinity.

Thus the fundamental theorem of algebra is a special case of this, the x-axis has degree \( 1 \) and any polynomial has degree \( d \) yielding \( d \) roots.

Any conic will then intersect the line at infinity at two points. We calculated this for specific conics already.

Any two conics will intersect at exactly 4 points.

1.1.12. Foundational Reason why we can extend the real numbers in this way

The main reason why we can do an extension logically is because the set of statements

\( x < 1, x < \frac{1}{2}, x < \frac{1}{3}, \cdots \) and \( x > 1, x > 2, x > 3, \cdots \)

Cannot create a contradiction in a first order language. Proofs are necessarily finitely long. Hence any proof of a contradiction would only use a finite number of those statements. But there are real numbers that are smaller than a finite number of rationals and bigger than a finite number of integers.

So the extensions will not cause a contradiction in the first order language of \( \mathbb{R} \).

You can get many such extensions, hyperreals, surreals, extended line (with 2 infinities), or projective line (1 infinity).

1.2. Definitions

1.2.1. Projective Space Formally

The projective space is the set of all linear subspaces of \( \mathbb{C}^{n+1} \).

\( \mathbb{P}^n = (\mathbb{C}^{n+1} - \{0\}) / \mathbb{C}^* \) where \( \mathbb{C}^* \) acts via homotheties \( \lambda (a_i) = (\lambda a_i) \)

This was motivated by having us stand at the origin and having rays of light act as our means of perspective.

1.2.2. Homogeneous Polynomials

Given any polynomial we can define the vanishing set.

The question is, in general, is the vanishing set well defined in projective space?

No. Example: \( p(x) = x^2 + 1 \) then \( V(p) = \{i, -i\} \). But \( i \in V(p) \implies 2i \in V(p) \) is false.

What will fix it is if we have the condition that \( p(x) = 0 \iff p(\lambda x) = 0 \). A good question is then, which polynomials satisfy this?

This is a fun exercise and it turns out that such polynomials are such that the degree of every term is the same.

1.2.3. Homogenization

Given any polynomial we have a reversible formula for how one may obtain a homogeneous polynomials

Let \( p \) be any polynomial in \( n \) variables with degree \( d \).

We may get a homogeneous polynomial \( ^hp \) from \( x_0^d p(\frac{x_1}{x_0}, \cdots, \frac{x_n}{x_0}) \)

For example, \( x^2 + 1 \mapsto x_0^2 ((\frac{x_1}{x_0})^2 + 1) = x_0^2 (\frac{x_1^2}{x_0^2}) + x_0^2 = x_1^2 + x_0 \)

The quick way to do this in your head is to multiply enough \( x_0 \)'s in each term to make the sum of the degrees add up to the max degree \( d \).

To get back to the original polynomial, set \( x_0 \) to \( 1 \).

  5. Projective Nullstellensatz

    Let \( R_+ = \bigoplus_{d \geq 1} R_d \) be the homogeneous ideal.

    Define \( I_\mathbb{P}^n(S) = \{f \in R_+ \vert \forall x \in S \colon f(x) = 0 \} \) where \( x \) is in homogeneous coordinates \( (a_0 \colon \cdots\colon a_n) \). This implies homogeneous components of \( f \) are also zero and that the ideal is homogeneous. Equivalantly, its generated by the homogeneous polynomials which vanish on \( S \). Define \( V_\mathbb{P}^n(I) = \{x \in \mathbb{P}^n \vert \forall f \in I \colon f(x) = 0 \} \)

    Then \( \sqrt{I} = I_\mathbb{P}^n(V_\mathbb{P}^n(I)) \) and There is an order-reversing one-to-one correspondence between proper homogeneous ideals of \( R \) and subsets of \( \mathbb{P}^n \) of the form \( V_\mathbb{P}^n(I) \). The correspondence is given by \( I_\mathbb{P}^n \) and \( V_\mathbb{P}^n \).

    Remark: The Nullstellensatz has many many generalizations. It's like the universe conspires to make the remark true about the correspondence between Algebra and Geometry.

    Serge Lang extends the Nullstellensatz to the case of infinitely many generators. Let \( \kappa \) be an infinite cardinal and \( K \) be algebraically closed field with transcendence degree (the cardinality of the largest algebraically independent subset) strictly greater than \( \kappa \) over its prime subfield. Then for any set \( S \) whose cardinality is \( \kappa \) the polynomial ring \( K[x_i]_{i \in S} \) satisfies the Nullstellensatz.

1.3.1. \( \mathbb{P}^n \) is a Toric Variety

  1. Torus

    The set, \( T_{\mathbb{P}^n} = \mathbb{P}^n \setminus \mathbf{V}(x_0 \cdots x_n) \) (Projective space where no coordinate is 0) is a Torus.

    \[ \begin{align*} T_{\mathbb{P}^n} &= \mathbb{P}^n \setminus \mathbf{V}(x_0 \cdots x_n) \\ &= \{(a_0, \ldots, a_n) \in \mathbb{P}^n \vert a_0 \cdots a_n \neq 0 \} \\ &= \{ (1, t_1, \ldots, t_n) \in \mathbb{P}^n \vert t_1, \cdots, t_n \in \mathbb{C}^* \} \\ &\simeq (\mathbb{C}^*)^n \end{align*} \]

  2. Torus Action

    \( \mathbb{T}_{\mathbb{P}^n} * \mathbb{P}^n \) is defined via \( [t_0 \colon \ldots \colon t_n][x_0 \colon \ldots \colon x_n] = [t_0 x_0 \colon \ldots t_n x_n] \) The action is well defined \( t [cx] = t[x], ct[x] = t[x] \) The action preserves zeros \( x_i = 0 \)

  3. Character Lattice of \( T_{\mathbb{P}^n} \)

    Recall: \( \forall m \in \mathscr{M}_n \ \exists \chi_m \colon \mathbb{T}^{N} \to \mathbb{C}^* \) via \( (t_i)_{i = 0}^{n} \mapsto \prod_{i = 0}^{n} t_i^{m_i} \)

    For projective varieties we have the following characterization: \( \mathscr{M}_n = \{(a_i)_{i=0}^{n} \in \mathbb{Z}^{n+1} \vert \sum_{i=0}^{n} a_i = 0\} \)

    \( \forall [t_i]_{i = 0}^{n} \mathbb{P}^n \exists k \in [0 .. n] . t_k \neq 0 \)

    Hence \( [t_i]_{i = 0}^{n} = [\frac{t_i}{t_k}]_{i = 0}^{n} \)

    \( \prod t_i^{m_i} = \chi_m([t_i]) = \chi_m([\frac{t_i}{t_k}]) = t_k^{-\sum m_i} \prod t_i^{m_i} \)

    Hence \( t_k^{-\sum m_i} = 1 \implies \sum m_i = 0 \)

    This is because if we have a point then \( \exists t_i \neq 0 \) \( [t_0 \colon \ldots \colon t_n] = [\frac{t_0}{t_i} \colon \ldots \colon 1 \colon \ldots \colon \frac{t_n}{t_i}] \)

    The character evaluated on the point is then \( t_0^{a_0} \cdots t_n^{a_n} = (\frac{t_0}{t_i})^{a_0} \cdots (\frac{t_n}{t_i})^{a_n} = t_i^{- \sum_i a_i} t_0^{a_0} \cdots t_n^{a_n} \) This implies \( t_i^{- \sum_i a_i} = 1 \) hence the sum is \( 0 \).

    The character lattice of \( \mathbb{T}_{\mathbb{P}^n} \) is given by \( \mathscr{M}_n = \{(a_i)_{i=0}^{n} \in \mathbb{Z}^{n+1} \vert \sum_{i=0}^{n} a_i = 0\} \)

    Recall: The character lattice for affine tori is a collection of functions Given some \( m \in \mathscr{M}_n \) we have a character function \( \mathbb{T}^N \xrightarrow{\chi_m} \mathbb{C}^* \) via \( (t_i)_{i = 0}^{n} \mapsto \prod_{i = 0}^{n} t_i^{m_i} \)

    This is because if we have a point then \( \exists t_i \neq 0 \) \( [t_0 \colon \ldots \colon t_n] = [\frac{t_0}{t_i} \colon \ldots \colon 1 \colon \ldots \colon \frac{t_n}{t_i}] \)

    The character evaluated on the point is then \( t_0^{a_0} \cdots t_n^{a_n} = (\frac{t_0}{t_i})^{a_0} \cdots (\frac{t_n}{t_i})^{a_n} = t_i^{- \sum_i a_i} t_0^{a_0} \cdots t_n^{a_n} \) This implies \( t_i^{- \sum_i a_i} = 1 \) hence the sum is \( 0 \).
