1.1. Problem 1

Let \( \phi(z) := z + 1/z \).

1.1.1. Holomorphic

Because \( z \) is holomorphic on \( \mathbb{C} \) and \( 1/z \) is holomorphic on \( \mathbb{C} - \left\{ 0 \right\} \) then \( \phi \) is holomorphic on \( \mathbb{C} \cap (\mathbb{C} - \left\{ 0 \right\}) = \mathbb{C} - \left\{ 0 \right\} \subset \hat{\mathbb{C}} - \overline{\mathbb{D}} \)

1.1.2. Non-vanishing Derivative

We may find the points in which the derivative vanish,

\[ \phi'(z) = 1 - 1/z^{2} = 0 \implies z^{2} = 1 \implies z = \pm 1 \]

Hence \( \phi' \) is nonvanishing when the domain is \( \hat{\mathbb{C}} - \overline{\mathbb{D}} \).

1.1.3. Injective

The inverse,

\[ \phi(z) = z + 1/z \implies z \phi(z) = z^{2} + 1 \implies 0 = z^{2} - z \phi(z) + 1 \]

Hence,

\[ z = \frac{\phi(z) \pm \sqrt{\phi(z)^{2} - 4}}{2} \]

So for every point in the image (except for \( \pm 2 \)) we have two such \( z \) which maps to it. This tells us that the map is surjective.

However, given the domain \( \hat{\mathbb{C}} - \overline{\mathbb{D}} \) for any two distinct \( z_{1} \) and \( z_{2} \), we have the inequalities

\[ \left| \frac{1}{z_{1}} - \frac{1}{z_{2}} \right| = \frac{|z_{2} - z_{1}|}{|z_{1}||z_{2}|} < |z_{2} - z_{1}| \]

Which tells us

\[ \frac{1}{z_{1}} - \frac{1}{z_{2}} \neq z_{2} - z_{1} \iff z_{1} + \frac{1}{z_{1}} \neq z_{2} + \frac{1}{z_{2}} \]

In other words, \( \phi(z) \) is injective (not double covered).

1.1.4. Image

We can look at the real and imaginary parts of \( \phi(z) \) given \( z = re^{i\theta} \).

\[ u = u + iv = r e^{i\theta} + \frac{1}{r} e^{-i\theta} = \left( r + \frac{1}{r} \right) \cos(\theta) + \left( r - \frac{1}{r} \right) i \sin(\theta) \]

Which tells us that circles are mapped onto ellipses. The only non ellipse is when \( r = 1 \) which yields the line segment \( [-2,2] \). Such an \( r \) is not in our domain, hence the image is \( \hat{\mathbb{C}} - [-2,2] \).

1.1.5. \( P(z) := z^{2} - 2 \)

We note that \( P \) is \( \phi \)-conjugate to \( z^{2} \),

\begin{align} P(\phi(z)) &= \left( z + \frac{1}{z} \right)^{2} - 2 \\ &= z^{2} + 2 + \frac{1}{z^{2}} - 2 \\ &= z^{2} + \frac{1}{z^{2}} \\ &= \phi(z^{2}) \end{align}

Hence,

\[ I_{P} = \phi(I_{z^{2}}) = \phi(\hat{\mathbb{C}} - \overline{\mathbb{D}}) = \hat{\mathbb{C}} - [-2,2] \]

So,

\[ K_{P} = [-2,2], J_{P} = \partial K_{P} = [-2,2] \]

Let \( \{ V_{n} \}_{n \in \mathbb{N}} \) be an increasing sequence of simply connected domains in \( \hat{\mathbb{C}} \) all containing \( \infty \). Denote \( V = \bigcup_{n} V_{n} \).

1.2.1. Proof of Connectedness of the Union

Consider a two set disjoint open cover, \( V \subseteq U_{1} \bigcup U_{2} \).

Because each \( V_{n} \) is connected, they must be fully contained in one of the \( U_{i} \)'s. Thus, \( V_{0} \subseteq U_{\lambda} \). Consider any other \( j \in \mathbb{N} \). \( V_{j} \subseteq U_{i} \). By disjointness and using the increasing sequence, we have that \( V_{0} \subseteq V_{j} \subseteq U_{\lambda} \).

Hence, for all \( n \in \mathbb{N} \), \( V_{n} \subseteq U_{\lambda} \) therefore the other open set is empty and the cover is not a disconnection.

1.2.2. Proof of Connectedness of the Intersection

The complement of \( \bigcup V_{n} \) is

\[ \hat{\mathbb{C}} - V = \hat{\mathbb{C}} - \left( \bigcup_{n \in \mathbb{N}} V_{n} \right) = \bigcap_{n \in \mathbb{N}} \left( \hat{\mathbb{C}} - V_{n} \right) = \bigcap_{n \in \mathbb{N}} W_{n} \]

Where

\[ W_{n} = \hat{\mathbb{C}} - V_{n} \]

is a decreasing sequence of compact sets (because \( V_{n} \) contains \( \infty \)). If \( W_{n} = \emptyset \) for some \( n \), then the theorem holds. Consider \( W_{n} \neq \emptyset \) for all \( n \), and suppose there is a disconnection of compact neighborhoods \( K_{1} \subset U_{1} \) and \( K_{2} \subset U_{2} \) of the intersection,

\[ \bigcap_{n \in \mathbb{N}} W_{n} \subseteq K_{1} \bigcup K_{2} \subset U_{1} \bigcup U_{2} \]

Where the intersection has points in both \( K_{1} \) and \( K_{2} \).

Because this is a sequence of sets, there is some \( i \) such that \[ W_{i} \subseteq K_{1} \bigcup K_{2} \subset U_{1} \bigcup U_{2} \]

then by Cantor's Intersection theorem we have that the intersection is nonempty. Hence we can partition \( W_{i} = (W_{i} \cap U_{1}) \bigcup (W_{i} \cap U_{2}) \) which is impossible by connectedness of \( W_{i} \).

1.10. Problem 10

Let \( \left\{ f_{n} \right\} \) be a normal family of holomorphic functions on \( \Omega \) and \( f(z) = \lim_{n \to \infty} f_{n}(z) \) exists pointwise on \( E \subset \Omega \) which has an accumulation point in \( \Omega \).

We will use the following characterization of limits,

\( f_{n} \to f \) if and only if every subsequence \( f_{n_{k}} \) has a further subsequence \( f_{n_{k_{j}}} \to f \).

Consider a subsequence \( f_{n_{k}} \). The subsequence forms a normal family by using the same local bound as the larger family \( f_{n} \). Hence, there is a subsequence with limit \( f_{n_{k_{j}}} \to g_{n_{k}} \). For any \( z \in E \) we can calculate the limits

\begin{align} f(z) &= \lim_{n \to \infty} f_{n}(z) \\ &= \lim_{k \to \infty} f_{n_{k}}(z) \\ &= \lim_{j \to \infty} f_{n_{k_{j}}}(z) \\ &= g_{n_{k}}(z) \end{align}

Because \( E \) contains an accumulation point, we note that \( f(z) = g_{n_{k}}(z) \) for all \( z \in \Omega \).
