Linear Algebra

1. Introduction

Linear algebra is the study of "linear spaces" and "linear transformations".

A common and effective problem solving technique is to take a difficult problem, split it up into more manageable parts, and combine all of the parts together. Linear spaces are spaces in which each point is decomposable into basic building blocks. Linear transformations are functions between linear spaces which play nicely with the decompositions. The decompositions are done with other mathematical objects which behave very closely to numbers. This means that oftentimes, the solutions to problems using linear algebra have an algorithmic or formulaic component and can be turned into a computer program.

8. \( A = LU \)   drill





Given a matrix \( A \).
How can one factor it into a lower triangular matrix \( L \) and an upper triangular \( R \)?







8.1. Solution





Row reduce \( A \) into an upper triangular matrix \( R \).
All row reductions are left multiplications by matrices \( E_{ij} \) whose product is a lower triangular matrix \( L \).











9. Column Space   drill





How does one find the column space of a matrix?







9.1. Solution





Take \( A \), row reduce it, find the pivots.
The basis of the column space are the unreduced pivots of \( A \).











10. Null Space   drill





How does one find the null space of a matrix?







10.1. Solution





Take \( A \), row reduce it, find the pivots.
The basis of the column space are the columns that are not pivots of \( A \).

13. \( A = QR \)   drill

13.1. Solution





Use the Gram-Schmidt algorithm to find an orthogonal basis and place the column vectors in \( Q \).

68. QR Factorization Stability





Suppose that we wish to compute the QR factorization of a matrix.



\begin{align}
f \colon A \mapsto (Q,R) \tag{Problem}
\end{align}



We can do this in three ways,



\begin{align}
\tilde{f}_{GS} &\colon A \mapsto (\tilde{Q},\tilde{R}) \tag{Gram Schmidt} \\
\tilde{f}_{MGS} &\colon A \mapsto (\tilde{Q},\tilde{R}) \tag{Modified Gram Schmidt} \\
\tilde{f}_{HH} &\colon A \mapsto (\tilde{Q},\tilde{R}) \tag{Householder}
\end{align}



We may now compare the stability properties of all of the algorithms with respect to the original problem, \( f \). To be backward stable, we want the output of our algorithm to be the exact output of a perturbed matrix: 





\[ \tilde{f}(A) = f(\tilde{A}) \]





The algorithm outputs \( Q \) and \( R \), and perturbing \( A \) yields \( A + \Delta A \) . Substituting these values means,





\[ (\tilde{Q}, \tilde{R}) = f(A + \Delta A) \]





This means that





\[ \tilde{Q} \tilde{R} = A + \Delta A \]





To show backward stability, we
