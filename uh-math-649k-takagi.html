1. Holzapfel-Gasser-Ogden Model

1.1. Scope and Importance

Blood transport is vital to all vertebrates including but not limited to us humans. Blood transports oxygen and nutrients to all cells throughout the body. Human tissue can only survive for four hours without proper blood flow. Blood is transported throughout the body using a circulatory system, first passing through the heart outwards via arteries then back to the heart via veins. Any part of the circulatory system is at risk of failure and the methods of treating such failure is complex. In order to gain a better grasp on possible treatments and pathologies of the circulatory system, we would like to understand how each component works from a mechanical perspective. The scope of this paper is to report on a constitutive model for arterial walls.

About 50% of all deaths in westernized society is caused by atherosclerosis. Atherosclerosis is a build up of plaque along arterial walls. One clinical treatment for atherosclerosis is percutaneous transluminal angioplasty (PTA). This involves inserting a catheter into a vessel and expanding a balloon at the end. Such a treatment requires a reliable constitutive model of the arterial wall in order to be safe. Risk of atherosclerosis can be minimized by healthily eating and exercise. However, arteriosclerosis risk happens due to age and is an unavoidable risk for elderly people. This makes understanding the arterial walls especially important.

The model developed can be applied to more than arterial walls. It falls into the regime of anisotropic hyperelasticity. Anisotropy means that it models materials that have different stress fields depending on its orientation. Hyperelastic means that the stress response comes from a strain energy function. This model is useful for many biomaterials, but also reinforced rubbers and plastics and composite materials.

1.2. Arterial Histology

In order to write a proper constitutive relation, we would need to know the properties of the constituents. Firstly, we simplify the model to only concern itself with in vitro effects. Hence, this model does not factor in vasa vasorum, nerve control, humoral control, pervascular connective tissue and neighboring organs like a pulsating heart. We also use many material parameters which will be estimated on an arterial section basis. We cannot say apriori if the carotid artery would have a different relationship or material parameters compared to the ulnar artery. In the model developed, we do make a simplifying assumption that they have the same governing equations and forms of stress, but leave comparison of material parameters for another paper.

Arterial walls have many parts which may be grouped at various levels of resolution. First, we consider two types of categories of artery: elastic vs muscular arteries. Elastic arteries have larger diameters and are located closer to the heart. Muscular arteries are the opposite, smaller diameters and located further away from the heart. Within the arteries there are three main layers, the intima, media, and adventitia. Within each of these layers we have sub layers.

The intima is the innermost layer consisting of a single layer of endothelial cells. Because of how thin this layer is in young healthy muscular arteries we consider it to have no contribution to the mechanics of the arterial wall. As we age, the intima does stiffen leading to arteriosclerosis. This is likely to have a significant impact on the mechanics. As mentioned earlier, atherosclerosis is a buildup of plaque in the intimal components. Because plaque can stick anywhere, this makes the geometry of this condition harder to model and its discussion is deferred.

The media is the middle layer and consists of a network of smooth muscle cells, elastin, and collagen fibrils.

The adventitia is the outermost layer and consists of fibroblasts and birocytes.

1.3. Description of the Deformation

The artery is a pre-stretched material under internal pressure load. This falls in the regime of large deformation anisotropic elasticity. To model the arteries, we take can use the elastic response of an anisotropic material based on an elastic free-energy function and specialize them for circular cylindrical geometry.

It is noted that arteries themselves are under stress. This can be seen when slicing them, the artery will spring open to form an open sector. For model simplification purposes we assume this radial cut leaves the artery unstressed. This is known to not be true (Vossoughi et al).

Let

  • \( \Omega_{0} \) be a fixed reference configuration of the continuous stress-free body.
  • \( \chi \colon \Omega_0 \to \mathbf{R}^{3} \) be the deformation map which transforms a material point, \( \mathbf{X} \in \Omega_0 \), to a deformed configuration \( \mathbf{x} = \chi(\mathbf{X}) \in \Omega \)
  • \( \mathbf{F}(\mathbf{X}) = \frac{ \partial \chi}{ \partial \mathbf{X}}(\mathbf{X}) \) be the deformation gradient
  • \( J(\mathbf{X}) = \det(\mathbf{F}(\mathbf{X})) > 0 \) be the local volume ratio

We may decompose the deformation gradient into its polar form, a spherical and unimodular part.

\[ \mathbf{F} = (J^{{1 / 3}} \mathbf{I}) \overline{F} \]

We may define the Cauchy-Green tensors \( \mathbf{C} \) and \( \mathbf{b} \) from the above.

\[ \mathbf{C} = \mathbf{F}^T \mathbf{F} = J^{(2/3) }\overline{\mathbf{C}}, \overline{\mathbf{C}} = \mathbf{\overline{F}}^T \mathbf{\overline{F}} \]

\[ \mathbf{b} = \mathbf{F} \mathbf{F}^T = J^{(2/3) }\overline{\mathbf{b}}, \overline{\mathbf{b}} = \mathbf{\overline{F}} \mathbf{\overline{F}}^T \]

In addition, we have the Green-Lagrange strain tensor \( \mathbf{E} \) and the modified strain tensor \( \mathbf{\overline{E}} \).

\[ \mathbf{E} = \frac{1}{2}(\mathbf{C} - \mathbf{I}) = J^{(2/3)} \overline{E} + \frac{1}{2}(J^{(2/3) }- 1) \mathbf{I}, \overline{\mathbf{E}} = \frac{1}{2}(\overline{\mathbf{C}} - \mathbf{I}) \]

1.4. Hyperelastic Stress Response

The paper describes the wall structure using a set of second order tensors

\[ \{ \mathbf{A}_{\alpha} \}_{{\alpha \in [1 .. n]}} \]

Which describe the fiber structure of the walls. In the case of arteries, this is one or two

And assume the existence of a decoupled Helmholtz free-energy function

\[ \psi(\mathbf{E}, \mathbf{A}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{A}_n) = U(J) + \overline{\psi}(\overline{\mathbf{E}}, \mathbf{A}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{A}_n)\]

Where \( U \) is a purely volumetric contribution and \( \overline{\psi} \) is isochoric (constant volume) contribution to the free energy. This gives us an idea of the types of motion of the wall given a constant temperature.

We can see the instantaneous dependence of free energy on the Cauchy Strain tensor by

\[ \frac{ \partial \psi}{ \partial \mathbf{E}} = \mathbf{S}_{\text{vol}} + \overline{\mathbf{S}} = \frac{ \partial U}{ \partial \mathbf{E}}(J) + \frac{ \partial \mathbf{\psi}}{ \partial \mathbf{E}}(\mathbf{\overline{E}}, \mathbf{A}_1, \ldots , \mathbf{A}_n) \]

Where \( \mathbf{S}_{\text{vol}} = \frac{ \partial U}{ \partial \mathbf{E}}(J) \) and \( \overline{\mathbf{S}} = \frac{ \partial \mathbf{\psi}}{ \partial \mathbf{E}}(\mathbf{\overline{E}}, \mathbf{A}_1, \ldots , \mathbf{A}_n) \)

We have the following results too,

\[ \frac{ \partial J}{ \partial \mathbf{E}} = J \mathbf{C}^{-1}, \frac{ \partial \overline{\mathbf{E}}}{ \partial \mathbf{E}} = J^{-2/3} \left( \mathbb{I} - \frac{1}{3} \overline{\mathbf{C}} \otimes \overline{\mathbf{C}}^{-1} \right) \]

Where \( \mathbb{I} \) is a fourth order tensor (a tensor with four components) defined by \( \mathbb{I}_{IJKL} = \frac{\delta_{IK} \delta_{JL} + \delta_{IL} \delta_{JK}}{2} \) where δ is the Kronecker delta. With these results above we may rewrite \( \mathbf{S} \) using hydrostatic pressure \( p = \frac{ d U}{ d J} \).

\[ \mathbf{S}_{\text{vol}} = p J \mathbf{C}^{-1}, \overline{\mathbf{S}} = J^{-2/3} \text{Dev}\left( \frac{ \partial \overline{\psi}}{ \partial \overline{E}}\right) \] Where \( \text{Dev}(\mathbf{T}) = \mathbf{T} - \frac{1}{3} [ \mathbf{T} \colon \overline{\mathbf{C}}] \overline{\mathbf{C}}^{-1}\) which has the property that \( \text{Dev}(\mathbf{T}) \colon \mathbf{C} = 0 \).

There is a Piola transformation which maps between Lagrangian and Eulerian coordinates and allows the Cauchy Stress tensor to be put in the decoupled form

\[ \sigma = J^{-1} \mathbf{F} \mathbf{S} \mathbf{F}^T = \sigma_{\text{vol}} + \overline{\sigma} \]

Where \( \sigma_{\text{vol}} = p \mathbf{I} \) and \( \overline{\sigma} = J^{-1} \text{dev}\left( \overline{\mathbf{F}} \frac{ \partial \mathbf{\psi}}{ \partial \overline{\mathbf{E}}} \overline{\mathbf{F}}^{T} \right) \)

Where \( \text{dev} \) is defined analogously,

\[ \text{dev}(\mathbf{T}) = \mathbf{T} - \frac{1}{3}[\mathbf{T} \colon \mathbf{I}] \mathbf{I} \] With the similar property that \( \text{dev}(T) \colon \mathbf{I} = 0 \).

In this paper, an artery is to be an incompressible thick-walled cylindrical tube which may be subjected to various loads. We may consider three separate but related configurations of the artery. One configuration is the load-free configuration, in which the artery is cut perpendicular to the radius. Another configuration is one in which the artery is cut along a radius, this is assumed to be stress free, though shown in other papers to not be quite true. The last configuration is the deformed configuration at some time along the deformation map.

The geometrical region of \( \Omega_0 \) using the above figure is,

\[ R_i \leq R \leq R_o, 0 \leq \Theta \leq (2 \pi - \alpha), 0 \leq Z \leq L \]

Where \( R_i, R_o \) are inner and outer radii, \( \alpha \) is the opening angle, and \( L \) is the length of the undeformed tube.

After deformation, the region \( \Omega = \chi (\Omega_{0}) \) is given by

\[ r_i \leq r \leq r_o, 0 \leq \theta \leq (2 \pi), 0 \leq z \leq l \]

Where \( r_i, r_o \) are inner and outer radii, and \( l \) is the length of the undeformed tube.

We assume \( \chi \) to be isochoric (constant volume) and is of the form

\[ \chi = r \mathbf{e}_r + z \mathbf{e}_z \]

With respect to basis vectors \( \{ \mathbf{e}_r, \mathbf{e}_{\theta}, \mathbf{e}_z \} \) in cylindrical polar coordinates \( (r, \theta, z) \)

Where

\[ r = \sqrt{\frac{R^2 - R_i^2}{k \lambda_z} + r_i^2}, \theta = k \Theta + Z \frac{\Phi}{L}, z = \lambda_z Z \]

With \( k = \frac{2 \pi}{2 \pi - \alpha} \) being the ratio of the tube opening angle in the unstressed configuration vs deformed configuration, and \( \Phi \) is the angle of twist arising from torsion. \( \lambda_{z} \) is a constant axial stretch, and is one of three principal stretches with the other two given by,

\[ \lambda_r(R) = \frac{ \partial r}{ \partial R} = \frac{R}{r k \lambda_z} \]

\[ \lambda_{\theta}(R) =\frac{r}{R}\frac{ \partial \theta}{ \partial \Theta} = \frac{kr}{R} \]

Note that these are stretches that occur when there is no twist. The stretch that occurs when there is twisting is given by \( \gamma \) which represents a locally the amount of shear in the \( (\theta, z) \)-plane. Its formula is given by,

\[ \gamma(R) = r \frac{ \partial \theta}{ \partial z} = r \frac{\Phi}{l} \]

The requirement that volume is preserved during deformation is that \( \lambda_r \lambda_{\theta} \lambda_z = 1 \).

We may use these expressions of the principal stretches to write out the deformation gradient, \( \mathbf{F} \), and the Cauchy-Green tensors \( \mathbf{C}, \mathbf{b} \). We note again that the deformation preserves volumes, which means that the tensors are equal to their rotational part in their respective polar decomposition.

\[ \mathbf{F} = \overline{\mathbf{F}} = (\lambda_{\theta} \lambda_z)^{-1} \mathbf{e}_r \otimes \mathbf{E}_R + \lambda_{\theta} \mathbf{e}_{\theta} \otimes \mathbf{E}_{\Theta} + \gamma \lambda_z \mathbf{e}_{\theta} \otimes \mathbf{E}_Z + \lambda_z \mathbf{e}_z \otimes \mathbf{E}_Z \]

\[ \mathbf{C} = \overline{\mathbf{C}} = (\lambda_{\theta} \lambda_z)^{-2} \mathbf{E}_R \otimes \mathbf{E}_R + \lambda_{\theta}^2 \mathbf{E}_{\Theta} \otimes \mathbf{E}_{\Theta} + \lambda_z^2(1 + \gamma^2) \mathbf{E}_Z \otimes \mathbf{E}_Z + \gamma \lambda_{\theta} \lambda_z (\mathbf{E}_{\Theta} \otimes \mathbf{E}_Z + \mathbf{E}_Z \otimes \mathbf{E}_{\Theta}) \]

\[ \mathbf{b} = \overline{\mathbf{b}} = (\lambda_{\theta} \lambda_z)^{-2} \mathbf{e}_r \otimes \mathbf{e}_r + (\lambda_{\theta}^2 + \gamma^2 \lambda_z^2) \mathbf{e}_{\Theta} \otimes \mathbf{e}_{\Theta} + \lambda_z^2 \mathbf{e}_e \otimes \mathbf{e}_e + \gamma \lambda_z^2 (\mathbf{e}_{\theta} \otimes \mathbf{e}_z + \mathbf{e}_z \otimes \mathbf{e}_{\theta}) \]

1.6. Equilibrium Equations

In the absence of body forces, we have the relation:

\[ \nabla \cdot \mathbf{\sigma} = \mathbf{0} \]

Due to the geometrical and constitutive symmetry the only nontrivial component of the equilibrium equation is:

\[ \frac{ d \sigma_{rr}}{ d r} + \frac{\sigma_{rr } - \sigma_{\theta\theta}_{}_{}}{r} = 0 \]

Using the boundary condition \( \sigma_{rr} \vert_{r=r_{0}} = 0 \) we may calculate the radial Cauchy stress \( \sigma_{rr} \) via

\[ \sigma_{rr}(\xi)= \int_{\xi}^{r_o} \frac{\sigma_{rr} - \sigma_{\theta \theta}}{r} dr\]

The internal pressure \( p_i = - \sigma_{rr} \vert_{r = r_i} \) is calculated similarly,

\[ p_i = \int_{r_i}^{r_o} \frac{\sigma_{\theta\theta} - \sigma_{rr}}{r} dr \]

If we know the state of deformation, we may calculate the axial force \( N \) and torsional couple \( M_{t} \) by the equations:

\[ N = 2 \pi \int_{r_i}^{r_{o}} \sigma_{zz}r dr, M_{t} = 2 \pi \int_{r_i}^{r_{o}} \sigma_{\theta z}r^2 dr \]

We may recast the expressions for \( N \) and \( M_t \) using the volumetric and isochoric split of \( \sigma \). That is, \( \sigma_{\theta\theta} = p + \overline{\sigma}_{\theta\theta} \) and \( \sigma_{rr} = p + \overline{\sigma}_{rr} \) and \( \sigma_{\theta z} = \overline{\sigma}_{\theta z} \). This allows us to write,

\[ N = 2 \pi \int_{r_i}^{r_o} \left[ \int_{r_o}^{\xi} \frac{\overline{\sigma}_{\theta\theta} - \overline{\sigma}_{rr}}{r} dr - \overline{\sigma}_{rr} + \overline{\sigma}_{zz} \right] \xi d \xi \]

Which leads to the general formula

\[ F = \pi \int_{r_i}^{r_o} (2 \overline{\sigma}_{zz} - \overline{\sigma}_{\theta\theta} - \overline{\sigma}_{rr}) r dr \implies F = N - r_i^2 \pi p_i \]

Which is very important as this is the quantity we may readily measure for the inflation tests on arteries.

1.7. Multi-Layer Model for Arterial Walls

The strain-energy function proposed takes in two layers for the wall corresponding to the media (M) and adventitia (A) layers of the artery. The walls are considered to be a composite reinforced by two families of collagen fibers arranged in symmetrical spirals. One assumption is to assume that the two layers have the same strain-energy function but have different material parameters. There is also an assumption of an additive split of the isochoric strain-energy function \( \overline{\psi} \) into isotropic and anisotropic deformations given by

\[ \overline{\psi}(\overline{\mathbf{C}}, \mathbf{a}_{01}, \mathbf{a}_{02}) = \overline{\psi}_{\text{iso}}(\overline{\mathbf{C}}) + \overline{\psi}_{\text{aniso}}(\overline{\mathbf{C}}, \mathbf{a}_{01}, \mathbf{a}_{02} ) \]

With \( \mathbf{a}_{01}, \mathbf{a}_{02} \) being direction vectors for the fibers each having magnitude \( 1 \). In accordance with the earlier definition of the strain-energy function depending on second order tensors, we define \( \mathbf{A}_{i} = \mathbf{a}_{0i} \otimes \mathbf{a}_{0i} \). Also, we use \( \overline{\mathbf{C}} \) rather than \( \overline{\mathbf{E}} \) for the deformation measure. To maintain frame invariance, we would like the strain-energy functions to depend on the invariants of combinations of our tensors. This forms what is known as an integrity basis, invariants \( \overline{I}_{1}, \ldots , \overline{I}_{9} \).

\begin{align*} \overline{I}_1 &= \tr \overline{\mathbf{C}} \\ \overline{I}_2 &= \frac{1}{2} \left( (\tr \overline{\mathbf{C}})^2 - \tr( \overline{\mathbf{C}}^2) \right) \\ \overline{I}_3 &= \det \overline{\mathbf{C}} \\ \overline{I}_4 &= \overline{\mathbf{C}} \colon \mathbf{A}_1 \\ \overline{I}_5 &= \overline{\mathbf{C}}^2 \colon \mathbf{A}_1 \\ \overline{I}_6 &= \overline{\mathbf{C}} \colon \mathbf{A}_2 \\ \overline{I}_7 &= \overline{\mathbf{C}}^2 \colon \mathbf{A}_2 \\ \overline{I}_8 &= (\mathbf{a}_{01} \cdot \mathbf{a}_{02}) \mathbf{a}_{01} \cdot \overline{\mathbf{C}} \mathbf{a}_{02} \\ \overline{I}_9 &= (\mathbf{a}_{01} \cdot \mathbf{a}_{02})^2 \end{align*}

This makes our problem into the following form:

\[ \overline{\psi}(\overline{\mathbf{C}}, \mathbf{A}_{1}, \mathbf{A}_{2}) = \overline{\psi}_{\text{iso}}(\overline{I}_{1}, \overline{I}_{2}) + \overline{\psi}_{\text{aniso}}(\overline{I}_{1}, \ldots , \overline{I}_{9}) \]

In order to reduce the total number of parameters, we make a simplifying assumption to only use \( \overline{I}_4 \) and \( \overline{I}_6 \) which are the squares of the stretching done in directions \( \mathbf{a}_{01} \) and \( \mathbf{a}_{02} \) respectively.

\[ \overline{\psi}(\overline{\mathbf{C}}, \mathbf{A}_{1}, \mathbf{A}_{2}) = \overline{\psi}_{\text{iso}}(\overline{I}_{1}) + \overline{\psi}_{\text{aniso}}(\overline{I}_{4}, \overline{I}_{6}) \]

We also make an assumption that \( \overline{\psi}_{\text{iso}}(\overline{\mathbf{C}}) \) follows a neo-Hookean model, hence,

\[ \overline{\psi}_{\text{iso}}(\overline{I}_1) = \frac{c}{2} (\overline{I}_{1} - 3) \]

With \( c > 0 \) is a stress-like material parameter. Because there is a strong stiffening effect of each layer, we take the anisotropic strain-energy function to be of exponential type.

\[ \overline{\psi}_{\text{aniso}}(\overline{I}_4, \overline{I}_6) = \frac{k_1}{2 k_2} \sum_{i = 4,6} \exp(k_2 (\overline{I}_i - 1)^2) - 1 \]

This gives enough information to derive an expression for the Cauchy stress tensor

\[ \overline{\sigma} = c \text{ dev} (\overline{\mathbf{b}}) + \sum_{i = 4,6} 2 \overline{\psi}_i \text{dev}(\mathbf{a}_i \otimes \mathbf{a}_i) \] With \( \overline{\psi}_4 = \frac{ \partial \overline{\psi}_{\text{aniso}}}{ \partial \overline{I}_{4}} \) and \( \overline{\psi}_6 = \frac{ \partial \overline{\psi}_{\text{aniso}}}{ \partial \overline{I}_{6}} \) and \( \mathbf{a}_{i} = \overline{\mathbf{F}} \mathbf{a}_{0i} \).

The free-energy functions in this case are:

\[ \psi_M = \frac{c_M}{2}(\overline{I}_1 - 3) + \frac{k_{1M}}{2k_{2M}} \sum_{i = 4,6} \exp(k_{2M}((\overline{I}_{iM}) - 1)^2) - 1, R_i \leq R \leq R_i + H_M \]

\[ \psi_A = \frac{c_A}{2}(\overline{I}_1 - 3) + \frac{k_{1A}}{2k_{2A}} \sum_{i = 4,6} \exp(k_{2A}((\overline{I}_{iA}) - 1)^2) - 1, R_i + H_M \leq R \leq R_o \]

Where the invariants are defined as:

\[ \overline{I}_{4j} = \mathbf{A}_{1j} \colon \overline{\mathbf{C}} \]

\[ \overline{I}_{6j} = \mathbf{A}_{2j} \colon \overline{\mathbf{C}} \]

The tensors \( \mathbf{A}_{1j} = \mathbf{a}_{01j} \otimes \mathbf{a}_{01j} \), \( \mathbf{A}_{2j} = \mathbf{a}_{02j} \otimes \mathbf{a}_{02j} \) can be written in cylindrical polar coordinate system with matrix notation

\[ \mathbf{a}_{01j} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ \cos(\beta_{j}) \\ \sin(\beta_{j}) \end{bmatrix}, \mathbf{a}_{02j} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ \cos(\beta_{j}) \\ -\sin(\beta_{j}) \end{bmatrix} \]

Where \( \beta_{j} \) are the angles between the collagen fibers.
