4.1.1. Constant Coefficient Transport

Take a Point \( x_{0} \), then the line through that point is \( \vec{x}_{0} + t \vec{v} \)

In 2D,

\begin{align*} \begin{bmatrix} x_{0} \\ x_{1} \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} t v_{0} \\ t v_{1} \end{bmatrix} &= \begin{bmatrix} x_{0} + t v_{0} \\ x_{1} + t v_{1} \end{bmatrix} \end{align*}

So,

\begin{align} x &= x_{0} + t v_{0} \\ y &= x_{1} + t v_{1} \end{align}

Solving for \( t \) then substituting gives and equation in the form \( y = mx + b \).

\[ y = x_{1} + \left( \frac{x - x_{0}}{v_{0}} \right) v_{1} \]

We may then move all of the initial conditions to one side. This will give a relation between \( x \) and \( y \) equaling some constant.

\[ - x_{1} + \frac{x_{0}v_{1}}{v_{0}} = \frac{v_{1}}{v_{0}} x - y \]

Equivalently,

\[ - v_{0} x_{1} + v_{1} x_{0} = v_{1} x - v_{0} y \]

Hence, in the case of PDEs where we have the equation

\[ \left\langle a , b \right\rangle \cdot \nabla V = 0 \]

We have characteristic lines

\[ \xi = bx - ay \]

4.1.2. Variable Coefficient Transport

\[ u_{x} + y u_{y} = 0 \]

Notice that this is the directional derivative dotted with the (variable) vector \( \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ y \end{bmatrix} \) That is, the equation is equivalent to,

\[ \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ y \end{bmatrix} \cdot \nabla u = 0 \]

This states that the function \( u \) is constant in the direction \( (1 , y) \). Thus, if we find curves which are everywhere tangent to \( (1,y) \) then \( u \) is constant on those.

4.1.3. \( u_t + b \cdot \nabla u = 0 \) - Linear Transport Equation

Let

  • \( b \in \mathbb{R}^n \) (be a constant vector)
  • \( u(t,x) \), \( t \geq 0 \), \( x \in \mathbb{R}^n \), \( \nabla = (\frac{\partial}{\partial x_1}, \cdots, \frac{\partial}{\partial x_n}) \)

Then consider \( u_t + b \cdot \nabla u = 0 \)

We may write this as \[ u_t + \sum_{i = 1}^n b_i \frac{\partial u}{\partial x_i} \] which is also equivalent to \( (u_t, \nabla u) \cdot (1, b) = 0 \)

Equation type:

  • Linear The equation is linear in the unknown.
  • First order The highest power of the differential is one.
  • Homogeneous Right hand side is identically \( 0 \).
  • Constant Coefficients Coefficients are \( 1 \) and \( b \) we may write \( (1,b) \in \mathbb{R}^{1 + n} \)

Notice that the function \( u \) has a directional derivative equal to \( 0 \) along \( (1,b) \).

Define a new function \( z \colon \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R} \) \( \)

\( z(s) = u(t + s, x+sb) = u( (t,x) + s(1,b)) \)

Then \( \dot{z}(s) = u_t(t+s, x+sb) + \nabla u(t + s, x + sb) \cdot b = 0 \)

So \( z \) is constant. This tells us that \( u \) is constant on the lines parallel to \( (1,b) \).

Denote \( \Sigma = \{ t = 0 \} \subset \mathbb{R}^{1+n} \) the "\( x \)" axis, \( \bar{u} \in C^1(\Sigma) \)

4.1.5. Method of Characteristics

This method finds curves in your space. Along such curves the PDE becomes a system of ODE's.

Here are some equations that can be solved using the method of characteristics:

\begin{align} \sum_{i = 1}^{n} a_{i}(x_{1}, \ldots, x_{n}, u) \frac{ \partial u}{ \partial x_{i}} &= c(x_{1}, \ldots, x_{n}, u) & \quad & \text{Quasilinear General} \\ 2 \frac{ \partial u}{ \partial x_{1}} + 60 \frac{ \partial u}{ \partial x_{2}} &= 0 & \quad & \text{Linear Homogeneous Constant Coefficients} \\ x_{1} \frac{ \partial u}{ \partial x_{1}} + x_{2} \frac{ \partial u}{ \partial x_{2}} &= 0 & \quad & \text{Linear Homogeneous Variable Coefficients} \\ x_{1}^{2} \frac{ \partial u}{ \partial x_{1}} + x_{2} \frac{ \partial u}{ \partial x_{2}} &= 0 & \quad & \text{Linear Homogeneous Variable Coefficients} \\ x_{1}^{2} \frac{ \partial u}{ \partial x_{1}} + x_{2} \frac{ \partial u}{ \partial x_{2}} &= x_{1}^{2} & \quad & \text{Linear Inhomogeneous Variable Coefficients} \\ x_{1}^{2} \frac{ \partial u}{ \partial x_{1}} + x_{2} \frac{ \partial u}{ \partial x_{2}} &= x_{1}^{2} + u & \quad & \text{Linear Inhomogeneous Variable Coefficients} \\ x_{1}^{2} \frac{ \partial u}{ \partial x_{1}} + x_{2} \frac{ \partial u}{ \partial x_{2}} &= x_{1}^{2} + u^{2} & \quad & \text{Semilinear Inhomogeneous Variable Coefficients} \\ x_{1}^{2} u \frac{ \partial u}{ \partial x_{1}} + x_{2} \frac{ \partial u}{ \partial x_{2}} &= x_{1}^{2} & \quad & \text{Quasilinear Inhomogeneous Variable Coefficients} \end{align}

The solution technique is:

  1. Turn the PDE into the standard quasilinear form.
  2. Solve the system of ODEs
\begin{align} \frac{ d x_{i}}{ d t} &= a_{i}(x_{1}, \ldots, x_{n}, z) \\ \frac{ d z}{ d t} &= c(x_{1}, \ldots, x_{n}, z) \end{align}

Let us solve:

\begin{align} x^{2} u_{x} + (x^{2} + y^{2})u_{y} = 5u \end{align}

Then:

\begin{align} \frac{ d x}{ d t} = x^{2} \\ \frac{ d y}{ d t} = y \\ \frac{ d z}{ d t} = 5 \end{align}

Hence:

\begin{align} x(t) = \frac{1}{C_{0} + t} \\ y(t) = C_{1}e^{t} \\ z(t) = 5t + C_{2} \end{align}

13. Rayleigh Quotient

Let \( S \colon U \to U \) be self adjoint linear operator on an inner product space. Define the Rayleigh quotient to be:

\[ R[u] = \frac{\langle u , S[u] \rangle}{||u||^2} \]

Theorem: Suppose \( u_{*} \) minimizes \( R[u] \). Then

  1. \( u_{*} \) is an eigenfunction of \( S \)
  2. \( \lambda_{*} \) the corresponding eigenvalue is the smallest eigenvalue of \( S \)

Proof: Suppose \( u_{*} \) minimizes \( S \). Consider the scalar function \( g(t) = R[u_{*} + t u] \) for any \( u \in U \)

\begin{align} g(t) &= R[u_{*} + tu] \\ &= \frac{\langle u_{*} + tu , S[u_{*} + tu] \rangle}{||u_{*} + tu||^2} \\ &= \frac{ \langle u_{*} , S[u_{*}] \rangle + 2t \langle u , S[u_{*}] \rangle + t^2 \langle u , S[u] \rangle}{||u_{*}||^2 + 2 \langle u , u_{*}\rangle + t^2||u||^2} \\ \end{align}

We know that because \( u_{*} \) minimizes \( S \), the minimum value will occur at \( t = 0 \).

By differentiating \( g(t) \) we yield

\[ 0 = g'(0) = \frac{2 ||u_{*}||^2 \langle u , S[u_{*}] \rangle - \langle u_{*} , S[u_{*}] \rangle ||u_{*}||^2}{||u_{*}||^4} \] \[ 0 = g'(0) = \frac{2 \langle u , S[u_{*}] \rangle - \langle u_{*} , S[u_{*}] \rangle}{||u_{*}||^2} \]

18. Integration By Parts

Recall that differential operators, \( \frac{ d }{ d x}, \nabla, \nabla \cdot, \nabla \times, \Delta, ... \) tend to obey a "product rule"

\[ \frac{ d (uv)}{ d x} = \frac{ d u}{ d x} v + u \frac{ d v}{ d x} \]

\begin{alignat*}{2} [\nabla (uv)]_{i} &= \begin{bmatrix} \frac{ \partial (uv)}{ \partial x_{i}} \end{bmatrix}_i &\quad &\text{$i$-th Component of $\nabla$}\\ &= \begin{bmatrix} \frac{ \partial u}{ \partial x_{i}}v + u\frac{ \partial v}{ \partial x_{i}} \end{bmatrix}_i &\quad &\text{$1$D Product Rule}\\ &= v \begin{bmatrix} \frac{ \partial u}{ \partial x_{i}} \end{bmatrix}_{i} + u \begin{bmatrix} \frac{ \partial v}{ \partial x_{i}} \end{bmatrix}_i &\quad &\text{Component-Wise Linearity} \\ &= v [\nabla u]_i + u [\nabla v]_i &\quad &\text{Def. $\nabla$} \end{alignat*} \begin{align*} &&[\nabla (uv)]_{i} &= \begin{bmatrix} \frac{ \partial (uv)}{ \partial x_{i}} \end{bmatrix}_i &\quad &\text{$i$-th Component of $\nabla$}\\ \implies &&[\nabla (uv)]_{i} &= \begin{bmatrix} \frac{ \partial u}{ \partial x_{i}}v + u\frac{ \partial v}{ \partial x_{i}} \end{bmatrix}_i &\quad &\text{$1$D Product Rule}\\ \implies &&[\nabla (uv)]_{i} &= v \begin{bmatrix} \frac{ \partial u}{ \partial x_{i}} \end{bmatrix}_{i} + u \begin{bmatrix} \frac{ \partial v}{ \partial x_{i}} \end{bmatrix}_i &\quad &\text{Thing} \\ \implies &&[\nabla (uv)]_{i} &= v [\nabla u]_i + u [\nabla v]_i &\quad &\text{Def. $\nabla$} \end{align*}

Each component matches, hence,

\[ \nabla(uv) = v \nabla u + u \nabla v \]

\begin{alignat*}{2} [\nabla (uv)]_{i} &= \begin{bmatrix} \frac{ \partial (uv)}{ \partial x_{i}} \end{bmatrix}_i &\quad &\text{$i$-th Component of $\nabla$}\\ &= \begin{bmatrix} \frac{ \partial u}{ \partial x_{i}}v + u\frac{ \partial v}{ \partial x_{i}} \end{bmatrix}_i &\quad &\text{$1$D Product Rule}\\ &= v \begin{bmatrix} \frac{ \partial u}{ \partial x_{i}} \end{bmatrix}_{i} + u \begin{bmatrix} \frac{ \partial v}{ \partial x_{i}} \end{bmatrix}_i &\quad &\text{Component-Wise Linearity} \\ &= v [\nabla u]_i + u [\nabla v]_i &\quad &\text{Def. $\nabla$} \end{alignat*}

Each component matches, hence,

\[ \nabla(uv) = v \nabla u + u \nabla v \]

19. Negative Laplacian is Self Adjoint

Given the linear operator

\[ L[u] = - u'' \]

with Boundary conditions

\[ u(0) = 0, u(1) = 0 \]

We have that \( L \) is self adjoint.

\begin{align} \left\langle L[u], v \right\rangle &= \int_{0}^{1} (-u''(x))(v(x)) dx \\ &= \Eval{v(x) (-u'(x))}{0}{1} - \int_{0}^{1} (-u'(x))(v'(x)) dx \\ &= \int_{0}^{1} (u'(x))(v'(x)) dx \\ &= \Eval{u(x)v'(x)}{0}{1} - \int_{0}^{1} (u(x))(v''(x)) dx \\ &= \int_{0}^{1} (u(x))(-v''(x)) dx \end{align}

21. Variational Formulation

Recall that the weak formulation of a PDE is given by:

\[ a(u,v) = \ell(v) \]

Where \( a \colon V \times V \to \mathbb{R} \) is a symmetric positive definite bilinear form.

We see that,

\[ J(v) = \frac{1}{2} a(v, v) - \ell(v) \]

\begin{align} \left. \frac{ d }{ d t} J(u + tv) \right|_{t=0} &= \left. \frac{ d }{ d t} \left( \frac{1}{2} a(u + tv, u + tv) - \ell(u + tv) \right) \right|_{t=0} \\ &= \left. \frac{ d }{ d t} \left( \frac{1}{2} a(u,u) + t a(u, v) + \frac{t^{2}}{2} a(v,v) - \ell(u) - t \ell(v) \right) \right|_{t=0} \\ &= \left. \left( a(u, v) + t a(v,v) - \ell(v) \right) \right|_{t=0} \\ &= a(u, v) - \ell(v) \end{align}

Hence we see that,

\[ \left. \frac{ d }{ d t} J(u + tv) \right|_{t=0} = a(u,v) - \ell(v) \]

Setting one side of the equation to zero proves the claim. That is, setting the LHS to zero means that \( u \) minimizes \( J \) implying \( a(u,v) = \ell(v) \). Setting the RHS to zero means \( a(u,v) = \ell(v) \) which implies \( u \) minimizes \( J \).

These minimizers are unique,

\[ a(u_{1} - u_{2}, v) = a(u_{1}, v) - a(u_{2}, v) = \ell(v) - \ell(v) = 0 \]

Hence \( u_{1} - u_{2} = 0 \).
