where

\[ F \colon \prod_{k} \mathbb{R}^{n^k} \to \mathbb{R} \] Then \( F \) is called a partial differential equation.

3. First Order Partial Differential Equations

3.1. Linear

3.1.1. \( u_t + b \cdot \nabla u = 0 \) - Linear Transport Equation

Let

  • \( b \in \mathbb{R}^n \) (be a constant vector)
  • \( u(t,x) \), \( t \geq 0 \), \( x \in \mathbb{R}^n \), \( \nabla = (\frac{\partial}{\partial x_1}, \cdots, \frac{\partial}{\partial x_n}) \)

Then consider \( u_t + b \cdot \nabla u = 0 \)

We may write this as \[ u_t + \sum_{i = 1}^n b_i \frac{\partial u}{\partial x_i} \] which is also equivalent to \( (u_t, \nabla u) \cdot (1, b) = 0 \)

Equation type:

  • Linear The equation is linear in the unknown.
  • First order The highest power of the differential is one.
  • Homogeneous Right hand side is identically \( 0 \).
  • Constant Coefficients Coefficients are \( 1 \) and \( b \) we may write \( (1,b) \in \mathbb{R}^{1 + n} \)

Notice that the function \( u \) has a directional derivative equal to \( 0 \) along \( (1,b) \)

\( z(s) = u(t + s, x+sb) = u( (t,x) + s(1,b)) \)

Then \( \dot{z}(s) = u_t(t+s, x+sb) + \nabla u(t + s, x + sb) \cdot b = 0 \)

So \( z \) is constant. This tells us that \( u \) is constant on the lines parallel to \( (1,b) \).

Denote \( \Sigma = \{ t = 0 \} \subset \mathbb{R}^{1+n} \) the "\( x \)" axis, \( \bar{u} \in C^1(\Sigma) \)
