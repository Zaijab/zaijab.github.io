  • \( x \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times k} \)
  • \( \theta \in \mathbb{R}^k \)

Given Consider \( x \theta \) over various conditions on \( \theta \).

Type Condition on \( \theta \) Condition on \( x \)
Linear \( \theta \in \mathbb{R}^n \) Linear Combination is in Space
Affine \( \mathbf{1}^T \theta = 1 \) Every Line given by two points is in Space
Conical \( \theta \succeq 0 \) If Every Ray starting from origin to point is in Space
Convex \( \mathbf{1}^T \theta = 1, \theta_i \succeq 0 \) If Every line segment between two points is in space

1.1. Affine Sets are Shifted Subspaces

Suppose we are given the affine space,

\[ \theta_1 x_1 + \cdots + \theta_k x_k \]

We know that if we have \( \theta_2, \ldots, \theta_k \) then we may solve for \( \theta_1 = 1 - \sum_{i = 2}^k \theta_i \)

\[ (1 - \theta_2 - \theta_3 - \cdots - \theta_k) x_1 + \cdots + \theta_k x_k \]

\[ x_1 + \theta_2 (x_2 - x_1) + \cdots + \theta_k (x_k - x_1) \]

This is a subspace shifted by \( x_1 \).

1.2. Affine Dimension

The affine dimension of a set is the dimension of the smallest affine space going through the set.

For example, the affine dimension of the line is \( 1 \), plane \( 2 \), and circle \( 2 \).

The relative interior of a set is the interior of the set in the affine space.

3. Examples

3.1. Solution Sets

A solution set for a matrix \( A \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}\) and vector \(b \in \mathbb{R}^{m} \) is

\[ S = \{ x | Ax = b \} \]

Theorem: Solution sets are affine spaces.

Proof: Suppose \( x_1, x_2 \in S \)

\begin{align} A (\theta x_1 + (1 - \theta) x_2) &= \theta A x_1 + (1 - \theta) A x_2 \tag{Matrix Mult. Linear} \\ &= \theta b + (1 - \theta) b \tag{Hypothesis} \\ &= b \tag{Calculation} \end{align}

3.2. Hyperplanes

A Hyperplane is

\[ H = \{ x \in \mathbb{R}^n \mid a^T x = b \} \]

Such a set is a solution set.

3.3. Cones

A cone is a set such that a positive linear combination remains in the cone.

Cones are Convex

Proof: Suppose \( x_1, x_2 \in C \) \[ \theta x_1 + (1 - \theta) x_2 \in C \] Because \( \theta \in [0,1], 1 - \theta \in [0, 1] \)

3.4. Halfspace

A Halfspace is the set

\[ H = \{ x | a^T x \leq b \} \]

This is a cone, hence convex.

Proof: Suppose \( x_1, x_2 \in H \), \( \alpha, \beta \in \mathbb{R}_{> 0} \) WLOG Shift half space to \( 0 \) \[ a^T (\alpha x_1 + \beta x_2) = \alpha a^T x_1 + \beta a^T x_2 \leq 0 \]

3.5. Polyhedra

A Polyhedron is the intersection of halfspaces.

It is then convex.

3.6. Simplicies

Given a collection of \( k+1 \) affinely independent points

(meaning: \( v_1 - v_0, \ldots, v_k - v_0 \) are linearly independent)

Take the convex hull of the points. This is necessarily convex.

3.7. Norm Balls and Cones

The Ball in metric \( d \), centered at 0, and having radius \( 1 \), \( B_d(0,1) \) is convex.

Proof:

Suppose \( x_1, x_2 \in B_d(0,1) \).

\begin{align} || \theta x_1 + (1 - \theta) x_2 || &\leq \theta || x_1 || + (1 - \theta) || x_2 || \tag{Triangle Inequality} \\ &\leq \theta + (1 - \theta) \tag{\( x_1, x_2 \in B_d(0,1) \)} \\ &= 1 \tag{Calculation} \end{align}

We will prove in later seminars that Affine Transformations Preserve convexity. Hence, translations, linear dilations of the ball will be convex. So the following are convex:

  • Ball of any radius
  • Ball with any center
  • Ellipses
