Measure Theory

1. Well Ordering and Measurability

1.1. The Well Ordered Relation is not measurable in \( \mathbb{R}^2 \)

This is the proof I am familiar with (from Rudin) and it uses CH. I've been told that CH can be avoided for this proof using a certain minimality argument but I haven't gotten around to proving that.

Let \( \prec \) be a well-ordering of the reals, \( \alpha < \omega_1 \), \( \mu \) denote Lebgesgue measure.

Define the following notation,

  • \( S_\alpha = \{ x_\beta \colon \beta < \alpha \} \)
  • \( S^\alpha = \{ x_\beta \colon \beta > \alpha \} \)
  • \( S_{x_\alpha} = S_{\alpha} \)
  • \( S^{x_\alpha} = S^{\alpha} \)

We may calculate \( \mu(\prec) \) using Fubini.

\( \mu(\prec) = \int \mu(S_y) dy \) which is \(0\) as any point has countably many predecessors. \( \mu(\prec) = \int \mu(S^x) dx \) has full measure. This is a contradiction

If you do not want to use Fubini, you can do the \( \mu(S_y) = 0 \) because countable argument twice to show that \( \mathbb{R}^2 = \prec \cup \succ \) has measure \(0\).

1.2. Bernstein Sets are not Measurable

Bernstein set is a subset of \( \mathbb{R} \) such that it neither it nor its complement contains a perfect set. We may use a well ordering of \( \mathbb{R} \) to show that Bernstein sets exist.

First, consider the perfect subsets of \( \mathbb{R} \)

\[ \mathcal{P} = \{ P_\xi \colon \xi < 2^{\aleph_0} \} \]

They can be coded by a real number using the Cantor-Scheme. We may well order both \( \mathcal{P} \) and each \( P_\xi \).

We may select elements \( x, y \) by transfinite recursion.

Pick \( x_0, y_0 \in P_0 \) and \( x_1, y_1 \in P_1 \) such that all elements are distinct.

Suppose we pick \( x_\beta , y_\beta \in P_\beta \) distinct from all other such \(x, y\) where \( \beta < \alpha \). Then \( P_\alpha - \bigcup_{\beta < \alpha} \{ x_\beta, y_\beta \} \) is nonempty as \( P_\alpha \) has cardinality continuum and less than continuum many points have been taken.

Let \( B = \{ x_\xi \colon \xi < 2^{\aleph_0} \} \) and \( A = \{ y_\xi \colon \xi < 2^{\aleph_0} \} \). This is a uniquely determined set once we are given a well ordering of \( \mathbb{R} \) and an ordering of the \( P_\xi \). By construction both sets do not contain perfect sets and \( A \subset \mathbb{R} - B \) hence \(B\) is a Berstein set.

Note that if \( X \subseteq \mathbb{R} \) has positive Lebesgue measure, then \(X \) contains a perfect set of positive measure. Hence \( B \) has nonempty intersection with any set of positive measure. Another result is that \( B \) and \( \mathbb{R} - B \) both have inner measure \( 0 \) hence are nonmeasurable.

5. Integration

Integration has shown its worth within the realm of Calculus, especially in its application to the other natural sciences. The basic definition of the integral is

\[ \int_{\Omega} f dx = \sup_{P} \sum_{i} f(x_{i}) | P_{i} | \]

Where \( P \) is a partition of the space \( \Omega \) which we may index over.

One arrives at a generalization of this type of integration by expanding both the types of partitions allowed, and the types of functions considered. Concretely, integration can find the weight of an object, the length of a line, or the surface area of a shape. In this concrete setting we use a "dimention agnostic" term, measure. Hence, in the abstract theory of integration we call the generalized partitions measurable sets and the generalized class of functions measureable functions.
