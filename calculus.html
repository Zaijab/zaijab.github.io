Calculus

Oftentimes, a problem is difficult due to its scale. An approach to solve problems is to try and solve smaller, "easier" versions of the problem, then reconcile all of the smaller solutions into a larger, complete solution. How shall we make a steak?

  1. Buy the ingredients
  2. Make the sauce
  3. Cook the steak

Each of these sub problems can be further broken down to a level one finds easier to manage.

This approach has a number of names depending on the context: divide and conquer in computer science, induction for well orders, composability in category theory. Here are some problems which are elegantly solved using the divide and conquer approach:

  • Sorting Lists
  • Tower of Hanoi
  • Mathematical Induction
  • Dynamic Programming (Auto differentiation)

When our problems contain a notion of continuity or infinity, it is harder to break the problem up. In the infinite regime, a finite partition yields classes which are usually isomorphic to the whole and no progress has been made. The "correct" analogue to the divide and conquer principle is then to divide the problem into the infinitesimal and solve the problem at the smallest of scales. We would then piece the complete solution in terms of the infinite collection of infinitesimal solutions. This has been called the infinity principal.

The document aims to talk about the infinity principal and develop a framework which can be applied to the physical sciences.

3. Line Through a Point

Take a Point \( x_{0} \), then the line through that point is \( \vec{x}_{0} + t \vec{v} \)

In 2D,

\begin{align*} \begin{bmatrix} x_{0} \\ x_{1} \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} t v_{0} \\ t v_{1} \end{bmatrix} &= \begin{bmatrix} x_{0} + t v_{0} \\ x_{1} + t v_{1} \end{bmatrix} \end{align*}

So,

\begin{align} x &= x_{0} + t v_{0} \\ y &= x_{1} + t v_{1} \end{align}

Solving for \( t \) then substituting gives and equation in the form \( y = mx + b \).

\[ y = x_{1} + \left( \frac{x - x_{0}}{v_{0}} \right) v_{1} \]

We may then move all of the initial conditions to one side. This will give a relation between \( x \) and \( y \) equaling some constant.

\[ - x_{1} + \frac{x_{0}v_{1}}{v_{0}} = \frac{v_{1}}{v_{0}} x - y \]

Equivalently,

\[ - v_{0} x_{1} + v_{1} x_{0} = v_{1} x - v_{0} y \]
