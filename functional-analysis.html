1.3. \( \mathcal{L}(X,Y) \) is Banach when \( Y \) is complete

Let \( (f_{n}) \subset \mathcal{L}(X,Y) \) be a Cauchy sequence. Then,

\[ \exists M \colon i, j \geq M \colon \| f_{i} - f_{j} \| < \varepsilon \]

Hence,

\[ \forall x \in X \colon \| f_{i}(x) - f_{j}(x) \|_{Y} < \varepsilon \]

Because \( Y \) is complete there is some \( y \) such that \( f_{n}(x) \to y \)

Let \( f(x) = \lim_{n \to \infty} f_{n}(x) \) (the pointwise limit). We must now show that \( f \in \mathcal{L}(X,Y) \).

\[ f(x + y) = \lim_{n \to \infty} f_{n}(x + y) = \lim_{n \to \infty} f_{n}(x) + f_{n}(y) = f(x) + f(y) \]

\( f \) is bounded because each \( f_{n} \) is bounded, hence \( \| f - f_{n} \| \leq \varepsilon \).

1.4. Linearity and Continuity Intuition

Consider the function space consisting of smooth functions, \( C^{\infty}([0,1]) \).

We note that the differentiation and integration maps are linear operators between these spaces:

\[ \frac{ d }{ d x} \colon f \to f' \]

\[ \int_{[0,1]} (\cdot) dx \colon f \to \int_{0}^{1} f(x) dx \]

We also note that the smooth function space is a normed space with the sup norm. Hence, we can now ask if the linear operators are continuous as well.

2. Flashcards

2.1. Why is \( L^{2} \) the only Hilbert space out of the \( L^{p} \) family?

For \( p \in \mathbb{R}_{\geq} - \left\{ 2 \right\} \), TFAE:

  • \( L^{p} \) is a Hilbert Space
  • \( \dim(L^{p}) \leq 1 \)
  • \( A \cap B = \emptyset \implies \mu(A) = 0 \lor \mu(B) = 0 \)

In other words, nontrivial \( L^{p} \) spaces are only Banach but not Hilbert.

2.2. How does the type of \( L^{p} \) space change for multiple values of \( p \)?

\( 0 < p < 1 \implies \) Metric Space ("Norm" is not subadditive) \( p = 2 \implies \) Hilbert Space \( p \neq 2 \implies \) Banach Space

2.3. Why is the dual space of a normed linear space always a Banach space?

Because \( \mathcal{L}(X,Y) \) is complete when \( Y \) is. As we always work over complete fields (\( \mathbb{F} = \mathbb{R}, \mathbb{C} \)), the dual space \( \mathcal{L}(X, \mathbb{F}) \) is complete.
