Topology

A topology is a way to describe the "nearness" of points. In the real world, paths in space are smooth. But what does it mean to travel in a smooth way? Also, what "things" can move smoothly?

Set theory only contains course methods of comparing different sets. Let us compare the line \( \mathbb{R} \) and the plane \( \mathbb{R}^{2} \) using set theoretic operations.

  • Cardinality

    We may show that \( \mathbb{R} \) and \( \mathbb{R}^{2} \) contain the same set of points in set theory.

  • Subsets

    By definition, \( \mathbb{R} \) contains real numbers whereas \( \mathbb{R}^{2} \) contains tuples of real numbers. This means they are different sets and do not contain the same "types" of elements. Thus neither is a subset of another. However, intuitively, we can imagine \( \mathbb{R} \) sitting inside of \( \mathbb{R}^{2} \) by being the \( x \)-axis for example.

Now let us look at some properties that seem intuitively different between the two sets. We will eventually make such notions precise.

  • Connectivity

Sets like \( \mathbb{R} \) and \( \mathbb{R}^{2} \) have the same number of points but notions like "connectivity" are evidently different. For example, one may "disconnect" \( \mathbb{R} \) by removing one point, however removing one point from \( \mathbb{R}^{2} \) still yields a "connected" space.

Nearness Without Metrics?

A metric is a method by which we may describe how far apart two objects are using a real number.

  • How far apart are 5 and 2? 3
  • How far apart are (1,1) and (3,5)? \( \sqrt{2^{2} + 4^{2}} = \sqrt{20} \)

We may even begin to ask questions like, how far apart are \( \sin \) and \( \cos \)? It's a delightful question. For those who have not taken a class on metric spaces, what would be your answer? Why do you feel like your answer is correct? And are there more answers? Asking this to a group of people would allow for a very fruitful discussion I hope.

Though, in this discussion about distances, we are caught up in assigning a real number to every pair of points. This is useful, we may leverage the nice order properties and completeness of the real numbers to talk about sequences. The calculation of being in close proximity is also quite easy. How can I make sure that 2 is within 5 units of 3? Just take their distance and compare.

There are unfortunately many issues with these distances.

Real Numbers

Our imaginations are unbelievably rich with the amount of spaces we can play around in. We can imagine walking around on the real numbers. Skipping around going from one point to the next. I can imagine running on the plane going from my house to my neighbors. But what about spaces in which the distances can become infinite? Mathematically we may describe this space as

\[ \aleph_{1} \times \mathbb{R} \]

Where we take the first uncountable ordinal and stick the real line on itself over and over again.

The distances can become so unbelievably large that a simple real number could not quantify the distance properly.

Symmetry

Imagine that I was riding on a train that only moved in one direction.

In space we can ride some of the unstable manifolds in one direction only.

On a slide, the reachable points are those with lower potential energy. Think about Mario going down the princess slide.

We can talk about what are the places I can actually stop at in my current position. Suppose it takes me one hour to get to my next stop. Then I could reasonably state that my distance to my next stop is one hour. However, because I cannot go backwards, my previous stop is unreachable. Perhaps I could say that the distance is infinity. However, metrics are by definition symmetric. I could not describe the set of reachable points in a one way path using a metric.

Positivity

Time has a negative metric signature. A surprising fact is that time runs in a different distance away from us.

Not Qualitative

Even when we do have a metric to work with, the types of spaces we would encounter and the types of operations we would perform would not play well with the metric. We can imagine a strip of paper. The distance between the two points would be the standard Euclidean distance. Imagine we glued the ends of the strip together. We would get a cylinder. The distance between the two points would be the closest path between them. However, for points close to the glue, their distance between two points would be extremely different. In fact, there is no way to predict using metrics alone that would tell us what two points would be close by each other.

Equivalent Notions

Like any deep notion, a topology can be described in a number of equivalent ways.

Open Sets

Given a set \( X \) we may prescribe what points are close to each other by subsets we claim have some "wiggle-room" around each point. Every point \( p \in X \) can wiggle in \( X \). Every point of the empty set \( p \in \emptyset \) can wiggle vacuously. Then we have relations of wiggle sets with each other.

Homeomorphism

\[f \colon X \to Y \] homeomorphism \[\iff\] \[f \colon X \to Y \] bijective, continuous, and has bijective inverse

Determining Homeomorphism

To exhibit a homeomorphism we must create a function such that it is bijective, continuous, and has a continuous inverse.

To show that two spaces are not homeomorphic we must show that one space has an invariant (something preserved under homeomorphism) which is not the same.

E.g. \[(0,1)\] and \[[0,1]\] are not homeomorphic. Compactness is preserved under homeomorphism but \[(0,1)\] is not compact and \[[0,1]\] is.

Geometric Simplices

A geometric simplex is the convex hull of a collection of given points \( \left\{ p_{i} \right\} \)

We may denote the simplex \( \sigma \) from the points \( \left\{ p_{i} \right\} \) as \( \sigma = [p_{i}] \)

Where \( x \in \sigma \iff x = \sum_{i} \lambda_{i} p_{i}, \sum_{i} \lambda_{i} = 1, \lambda_{i} \geq 0 \)

Geometric Simplicial Complex

A Geometric Simplicial Complex is a collection of simplices where intersection happens at a face.

Abstract Simplices

An abstract simplex is a finite collection of points.

Abstract Complex

An abstract simplicial complex is a collection of simplices such that their intersection is in the complex.

General Position of Points

A collection of points \( \{ p_{i} \} \) are in general position if they are affinely independent.

So,

\[ \sum_{i} \lambda_{i} p_{i} = 0, \sum_{i }\lambda_{i} = 0 \iff \lambda_{i} = 0 \]

#+BEGINTHEOREM[General Position is Easy to get Into]

If \( \{ p_{i} \}_{i \in [1 \ldots n]} \) are a collection of distinct points, then there are \( \left\{ a_{i} \right\}_{i \in \left[ 1 \ldots n \right]} \) within \( \varepsilon \) distance away from all points and are in general position.

#+ENDTHEOREM

Barycentric Coordinates

Given a point \( x \in \sigma = [p_{i}] \)

Then \( x = \sum_{i} \lambda_{i} p_{i} \), we call \( (\lambda_{i})_{i} \) the barycentric coordinates of \( x \).

Barycentric Subdivision

Given a geometric simplicial complex \( K \), a simplicial complex \( K' \) is said to be a subdivision of \( K \) if:

  • \( \sigma \in K' \implies \sigma \subseteq \tau \in K \)
  • \( \tau \in K \implies \tau = \cup_{\text{Finite}, \sigma_{i} \in K} \sigma_{i} \)

The subdivision imply that \( K \) and \( K' \) are equal as sets and topological spaces. Only the simplicial structure changes.
