1. Grammar

1.1. Sentence Structure

Every sentence in Japanese has a subject (sometimes implicit) and a verb or adjective (always explicit).

Subjects are marked with 「が」 and verbs / adjectives will conjugate (change form) depending on the context of the sentence (happened in the past, did not happen, etc).

Sentences can end with verbs (A does B), nouns (A is B), or adjectives (A is B). Here are some sentences which describe a girl Sakura (桜).

Grammar Mora Example Translation
Noun だ 桜が日本人だ。 Sakura is Japanese.
Adjective い 桜が可愛い。 Sakura is cute.
Verb う 桜が歩く。 Sakura walks.

In all of these sentences, we have a subject (桜) marked by the particle が. In instances where the subject is obvious from context we may omit the subject entirely from the sentence.

1.1.1. Omission From Sentences

Hypothetically, imagine a language a language similar to English. One that has the same words, comma rules, and verb conjugations, but with one big difference. Imagine that at the start of every sentence, we needed to describe time we are intending the statement we say to take place. For example,

I am going to school. → Tomorrow, I am going to school. I need to eat. → In an hour, I need to eat. Math was cool. → During primary school, math was cool.

What about statements which are assumed to be true for all time?

I love math. → For all time, I love math. I hated that teacher. → From then on, I hated that teacher.

Sure, the information can be useful, but the extra information can sometimes lead to the sentences feeling cluttered and having not enough emphasis on the important things. The extra words can feel sluggish and despite you not needing it to get your point across, you would be ungrammatical to not have it.

1.4. Adjectives

Adjectives describe the nature of a noun. Adjectives can modify the noun directly (attributive form) or afar (predicative form). They can be conjugated for past / non-past tense along with being of positive / negative form.

  Positive Negative
Past 寒い 寒くない
Non-Past 寒かった 寒くなかった

1.5. Verbs

All Verbs end with an 「う」 noise

Verb Last Mora Meaning
する る Do
くる る Come
よむ む Drink
たべる る Eat

There are three verb classes, ichidan verbs, godan verbs, and irregular verbs. They differ in the way they conjugate. Ichidan verbs all end in 「る」 and the rule is always to remove the final 「る」 then to

Verbs use the stem system. This is a method by which you can start from one form of a verb (typically the plain non-past tense) and inflect based on meaning and intent of the verb. Politeness Tense Negativity Copula
