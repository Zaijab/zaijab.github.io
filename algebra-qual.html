3. Qual F21 Group Theory 1   drill

\(\lambda\)

  • \(G\) be a finite group with odd order

Can there exist \(e \neq x \in G\) be such that \(x = a x^{-1} a^{-1}\) (\(x\) and \(x^{-1}\) are conjugate)

3.1. Solution

No. BWOC: Suppose \(x \equiv x^{-1}\)

Note, \(x\) cannot be equal to its inverse: \(x = x^{-1} \implies x^2 = 1 \implies 2 \mid |G|\) But, \(2 \nmid |G| \implies x \neq x^{-1}\)

Thus, \(|C(x)|\) the size of the conjugate class of \(x\) is odd. If x inverse is not in the conjugate class we are done. Otherwise there are two other elements, meaning at least one that is not \(x^{-1}\), call it \(y\). Because \(x \equiv x^{-1}\) then \(y \equiv y^{-1}\), so every element in the conjugacy class of \(x\) also has its inverse within the conjugacy class of \(x\). This means \(|C(x)|\) is even; a contradiction.

8. Rings, Matrices and Ideals

Let

  • \( R \in \mathbf{Ring} \)
  • \( M_n(-) \colon \mathbf{Rng} \to \mathbf{Rng} \) be the endofunctor from rings without identity to \( n \times n \) matrices with elements in \( R \in \mathbf{Rng} \).

Show there is a bijection between the two sided ideals of \( R \) and \( M_n(R) \).

We may try to solve this problem in a way that is similar to the proof of Hilbert's Basis Theorem. In HBT we used the leading terms of the polynomials to generate the ideal. The idea for this problem is to use one element (the upper leftmost entry) of the matrix to generate the ideal.

Before I do the solution, a remark.

Denote \( E_{ij} \) denote the standard matrix unit, this is a matrix whose \( ij \)-th entry is \( 1 \) and \( 0 \) elsewhere. These matrices follow nice multiplication rules, namely, \( E_{ij} A E_{kl} = A_{jk} E_{il} \) for any matrix. That is, given a matrix, we can "select" any entry with coordinate \( jk \) and place it in the \( il \)-th coordinate using multiplication. This will play nicely with ideals.

We will show \( M_n(-) \) is a bijection of ideals.

  • Injectivity of \( M_n(-) \)

Simple, \( I \trianglelefteq R \implies M_n(I) \trianglelefteq M_n(R) \).

  • Surjectivity of \( M_n(-) \)

Let \( J \trianglelefteq M_n(R) \). We need to construct an ideal \( I \trianglelefteq R \) such that \( J = M_n(I) \). Claim, \( I = \{ A_{11} \vert A \in J \} \) is an ideal and it is the ideal we want.

  • \( I \trianglelefteq R \)

Let \( r \in R \) and \( i \in I \). Then \( ri = rA_{11} \) for some \( A \in J \). We know that \( J \) is an ideal, hence \( (rI_n)A \in J \) where \( ri \) is now the top left element of that matrix.

  • \( J = M_n(I) \)

By containment.

  • \( J \subseteq M_n(I) \)

Using the above remark, if \( A \in J \) then \( E_{1i}AE_{ji} = a_{ij} E_{11} \in J \) hence every such \( a_{ij} \in I \) so \( J \subseteq M_n(I) \).

  • \( J \supseteq M_n(I) \)

If \( i \in I \) then \( i = A_{11} \) for some \( A \in J \), hence \( rE_{ij} = E_{i1} A E_{j1} \in J \). Hence, given \( A \in M_n(I) \) \( A = \sum_{i,j} A_{ij} E_{ij} \in J \).

This problem uses the fact that the ideal is two-sided. We cannot generalize this to one sided ideals. A counter example to this is if we consider \( n \geq 2 \). The set of matrices whose columns from \( 2 \) to \( n \) are \( 0 \) forms a left ideal but not a right ideal. This ideal does not arise from the above construction.

Surprisingly, the ideals in the matrix world is the same as the matrices in the ring world. The ideal is only generated by one element too. Despite all the extra dimensions we are allowed to work with in matrix world, we only use one (as if there was no difference between working with the ring by itself, the one dimensional object). This is no coincidence, \( R \) and \( M_n(R) \) are Morita Equivalent. Matrices are a rose by another name, yet smell just as sweet, categorically speaking.
