3. Qual F21 Group Theory 1   drill

\(\lambda\)

  • \(G\) be a finite group with odd order

Can there exist \(e \neq x \in G\) be such that \(x = a x^{-1} a^{-1}\) (\(x\) and \(x^{-1}\) are conjugate)

3.1. Solution

No. BWOC: Suppose \(x \equiv x^{-1}\)

Note, \(x\) cannot be equal to its inverse: \(x = x^{-1} \implies x^2 = 1 \implies 2 \mid |G|\) But, \(2 \nmid |G| \implies x \neq x^{-1}\)

Thus, \(|C(x)|\) the size of the conjugate class of \(x\) is odd. If x inverse is not in the conjugate class we are done. Otherwise there are two other elements, meaning at least one that is not \(x^{-1}\), call it \(y\). Because \(x \equiv x^{-1}\) then \(y \equiv y^{-1}\), so every element in the conjugacy class of \(x\) also has its inverse within the conjugacy class of \(x\). This means \(|C(x)|\) is even; a contradiction.
